# Melody Writing Guide

Melody is the most remembered part of any piece of music. It is the line a listener walks away humming, the phrase that gets stuck in their head, the thing that makes a song feel inevitable once they have heard it. Writing a strong melody is part craft, part intuition — and the craft side can be learned, practiced, and developed. This guide breaks down the core techniques of melody writing into actionable concepts you can put to use immediately.

---

## Melodic Contour

Contour is the shape of a melody when you look at it from a distance — the overall pattern of ups and downs. Before you think about specific notes, think about shape.

### The Four Basic Contour Types

**Arch contour** rises to a peak and then comes back down. This is one of the most satisfying and universally used shapes because it mirrors natural speech patterns, tension and release, and the arc of a story. Many great vocal hooks follow an arch — they build anticipation as they rise, then feel like resolution as they descend. Try sketching a melody that peaks around the fifth or sixth bar of an eight-bar phrase.

**Ascending contour** builds steadily upward, creating urgency, excitement, or a sense of striving. Ascending lines feel unresolved on their own, which makes them effective in verses that are setting up a chorus payoff.

**Descending contour** feels like release, relaxation, or resignation. Many chorus endings and final phrases use descending lines to signal closure. A melody that ends by stepping or falling down to the tonic feels conclusive and grounded.

**Static contour** stays in a narrow range, creating tension through repetition or a hypnotic, insistent quality. Rap flows, chant-like hooks, and certain groove-based pop melodies lean on static contour deliberately. The tension in a static melody comes from harmony and rhythm shifting underneath it.

### Practical Tips for Contour

- Sketch the contour of a melody on paper before you write the actual notes. Just draw a line showing where it goes up and down.
- Contrast contours across sections. If your verse is relatively static, push the chorus into an arch or ascending shape to create lift.
- The highest note of a melody is a structural event. Place it consciously — usually near the emotional climax of the lyric.
- Avoid random zigzagging with no overall shape. Even if a melody has lots of internal movement, the big-picture contour should feel intentional.

## Intervals in Melodies

An interval is the distance between two notes. The intervals you choose define the emotional character of your melody as much as any other single factor.

### Stepwise Motion

Moving by half steps or whole steps (minor seconds and major seconds) is called stepwise or conjunct motion. This is the backbone of most singable melodies. Stepwise movement is:

- Easy to sing and remember
- Smooth and conversational in feel
- Effective at building or releasing tension gradually

The melody to "Yesterday" by Lennon and McCartney is predominantly stepwise. So are most folk melodies, hymns, and ballads. If you want something singable and emotionally direct, lean on steps.

### Leaps and Their Emotional Qualities

A leap (any interval larger than a step) introduces drama, surprise, or energy. Each interval has its own emotional flavor:

**Minor third (3 half steps):** Gentle, slightly melancholic. Very natural-sounding. Common in folk and children's melodies.

**Major third (4 half steps):** Bright and open. Feels optimistic without being dramatic.

**Perfect fourth (5 half steps):** Strong and assertive. Has a horn-call or fanfare quality. The opening of "Here Comes the Bride" or the first two notes of "Amazing Grace."

**Perfect fifth (7 half steps):** Noble, open, powerful. Used for heroic or epic moments. Think of the opening of Beethoven's Fifth Symphony, or the "Star Wars" main theme.

**Minor sixth (8 half steps):** Yearning, romantic, emotionally charged. One of the most expressive leaps in tonal music. The opening of "My Bonnie Lies Over the Ocean."

**Major sixth (9 half steps):** Warm and soaring. Bright version of the minor sixth. Think of "My Favorite Things."

**Octave (12 half steps):** Dramatic and declarative. Announces itself. The beginning of "Somewhere Over the Rainbow" is a famous octave leap.

### Balancing Stepwise Motion and Leaps

The classic principle: after a large leap, resolve stepwise in the opposite direction. This gives the melody a sense of balance and makes it easier to follow. A leap grabs attention; the stepwise resolution grounds it.

Practical example: leap up a sixth, then step down by half and whole steps back into the phrase. This is a shape you will find in countless melodies across centuries of music.

Too many consecutive leaps in the same direction create angular, difficult-to-follow lines. Too much purely stepwise motion can feel predictable or static. The most compelling melodies mix both.

## Tension and Release in Melody

Melody is a conversation between tension and release. Without tension there is no emotional pull; without release there is no satisfaction.

### Creating Melodic Tension

- **Dissonance against the harmony:** notes that clash with the underlying chord create tension. Scale degrees 2, 4, and 7 are natural tension notes against a tonic chord.
- **High register:** the higher a note sits in a vocalist's range, the more effortful and tense it sounds. Climactic high notes work because they carry physical and emotional strain.
- **Long held notes:** sustaining a note that is not the tonic — especially over a changing harmony — creates harmonic tension.
- **Rhythmic displacement:** landing on a beat where the listener expects resolution but then withholding it by skipping to a different note.
- **Chromatic movement:** half-step motion that implies notes outside the key creates instability.

### Creating Melodic Release

- **Landing on chord tones:** especially the root (1), third (3), or fifth (5) of the current chord.
- **Stepwise descent to the tonic:** a classic cadential gesture. Falling to the root note from a step above feels like arriving home.
- **Lower register:** moving melodically downward after a climax signals relaxation.
- **Longer note values on stable tones:** holding a consonant note lets the ear settle.

### Practical Application

Think of your melody in units of tension and release. A phrase that rises to a tense note and then resolves down creates a mini arc. String several of these together with increasing overall tension across the section, then release at the end of the chorus or section. This is the architecture of emotional storytelling in melody.

## Hook Writing and Memorability

A hook is the part of a song that catches and holds the listener. It is usually the most repeated melodic phrase, and it is designed to stick.

### What Makes a Hook Memorable

**Repetition with variation:** a hook uses a core melodic or rhythmic idea and returns to it reliably, but small variations keep it fresh. The variation can be harmonic (same melody over different chords), rhythmic (same pitches in a slightly different rhythm), or ornamental.

**Distinctive rhythm:** rhythm is often more central to memorability than pitch. Clap the rhythm of a great hook and you will recognize it. Syncopation, unexpected rests, and rhythmic anticipation all make a hook feel alive and distinctive.

**A "turn" or unexpected moment:** the best hooks often have one moment that surprises — a note slightly higher than expected, a rhythm that lands differently, a chromatic inflection. This element of surprise is what makes the hook interesting to return to repeatedly.

**Singability:** a hook should be easy enough for anyone to sing after hearing it once or twice. Keep the range moderate, lean on stepwise motion, and avoid overly complex rhythms.

**Brevity:** hooks are usually short — two to four bars. They state their idea quickly and completely.

### Practical Hook-Writing Tips

- Write the hook first, then build the rest of the song around it.
- Sing your hook out loud and see if you are still singing it two hours later. If you are not, the listener will not be either.
- Try writing the same melody idea ten different ways rhythmically before committing. Often the first rhythmic idea is the most obvious; the second or third pass reveals something more distinctive.
- The title of the song is often the lyric on the strongest melodic moment. Make sure the melodic emphasis aligns with the lyrical emphasis.

## Call and Response Patterns

Call and response is one of the oldest structural devices in music, found in African music, blues, gospel, jazz, rock, and pop. It works by dividing a musical phrase into two parts: a "call" that poses a musical question, and a "response" that answers it.

### How It Works Melodically

The call typically ends on an unstable note or an upward inflection, creating a sense of incompleteness. The response completes the thought, often landing on a more stable pitch.

This structure maps directly onto lyrical question-and-answer patterns, but it also works purely instrumentally. A guitar riff can call; a second guitar voice responds. A vocal phrase calls; the horn section responds.

### Using Call and Response in Song Sections

In a verse, the vocal melody might call and the rhythm guitar or keyboard fills the response space. In a chorus, the lead vocal might call with the hook, and backing vocals echo or complete the response. This creates conversation and space within the arrangement.

A classic blues approach: the vocal sings a one- or two-bar phrase (call), then there is a one- or two-bar instrumental break (response), then the vocal continues. This pattern gives the melody room to breathe and gives instrumentalists a place to interact with the singer.

### Practical Tips

- Try writing a melody where the first two bars are the call and bars three and four are the response. See if you can make the response feel like it genuinely answers the call.
- The response does not need to copy the call — it just needs to feel like a reply. Different contour, different rhythm, but completing the emotional idea.
- Call and response works across arrangement layers, not just within a single melodic line. Vocals call; instruments respond. Think of it as conversation.

## Melody-Harmony Relationship

Understanding how your melody relates to the underlying chords is essential for controlling the emotional effect of every note.

### Chord Tones

Chord tones are the notes that belong to the current chord (root, third, fifth, and seventh). Landing on chord tones feels stable, consonant, and resolved. Most melodic phrases begin and end on chord tones for this reason.

### Passing Tones

Passing tones are non-chord tones that move stepwise between two chord tones. They are brief and create smoothness and forward motion. A melody stepping from the root up to the third through the second degree is using a passing tone. Passing tones are harmonically mild — they create only gentle color.

### Neighbor Tones

A neighbor tone is a non-chord tone one step above or below a chord tone, which then returns to that chord tone. It creates a gentle ornamental tension and release around a stable note. Many melodic embellishments are neighbor tones.

### Tension Notes (Avoid Notes and Color Tones)

Some notes clash more significantly with the chord. Scale degree 7 against a major tonic chord, or scale degree 4 against a major tonic — these create dissonance that demands resolution. Used deliberately and resolved correctly, these tension notes create the most emotionally charged moments in a melody.

Extended harmony notes (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) placed in the melody over matching extended chords create a lush, sophisticated color without true dissonance. Jazz and R&B melodies lean heavily on these relationships.

### Practical Application

- Map your melody against the chord changes and identify every note as a chord tone, passing tone, or tension note. If all your melody notes are chord tones, the result will be predictable. Mix in passing tones and planned tension notes.
- Plan where your tension notes resolve. A tension note that resolves correctly is satisfying; one that drifts without resolution is confusing.
- Experiment with the same melody over different harmonies. The same pitch that is a consonant third over one chord becomes a dissonant minor seventh over another. Chord substitution can dramatically change the emotional feel of a melody without changing a single melodic note.

## Motif Development

A motif is a short, distinctive melodic or rhythmic idea — usually two to five notes — that serves as a building block. Great melodies are often built from the transformation and development of a single small motif.

### Core Development Techniques

**Repetition:** state the motif again unchanged. Simple repetition reinforces recognition and creates expectation. The power is in how you eventually vary it.

**Sequence:** repeat the motif starting on a different pitch, preserving the same interval relationships. A sequence that steps down (or up) creates a sense of inevitability and forward motion. Many classical and pop melodies are built almost entirely from sequences.

**Inversion:** flip the motif upside down. If the original leaps up a fourth and then steps down, the inversion leaps down a fourth and steps up. Inversion creates contrast while maintaining recognizability.

**Augmentation:** stretch the motif by making the note values longer (twice as long, for example). The same shape in half time feels slower, more weighty, or more dramatic.

**Diminution:** compress the motif by making the note values shorter. The same shape in double time feels more urgent, energetic, or playful.

**Retrograde:** play the motif backward. This is a more abstract transformation and is less common in popular music, but it can create interesting effects in instrumental contexts.

**Fragmentation:** take only one part of the motif — maybe just the first two notes — and develop that fragment. This is a powerful way to build intensity toward a climax.

### Practical Motif Writing Tips

- Start with a motif that has a clear rhythmic and melodic identity. Three or four notes with a distinctive rhythm and at least one interesting interval is enough to build an entire melody.
- Write your motif, then write five variations of it using the techniques above. The best version might be variation three or four.
- Listen to the opening of Beethoven's Fifth Symphony. Four notes. Three G's and an E-flat. That entire first movement is built from the development of that motif. The principle works at every scale of music.

## Range and Tessitura Considerations

Range is the span from the lowest to the highest note in a melody. Tessitura is where the melody sits most of the time — the "comfortable zone."

### Vocal Range Considerations

For untrained singers, a comfortable range is roughly an octave to a tenth. Writing much beyond a tenth makes the song difficult for most people to sing. If you are writing for a specific vocalist, know their range and write in it.

Tessitura matters as much as range. A melody that technically stays within someone's range but sits mostly at the top of it will be tiring and strained. A melody that sits in the middle of the voice, with occasional peaks to the top, will feel powerful without being exhausting.

Typical comfortable ranges by voice type:
- Soprano: C4 to G5 (middle C up two octaves)
- Mezzo-soprano: A3 to F5
- Alto: F3 to D5
- Tenor: B2 to G4
- Baritone: G2 to E4
- Bass: E2 to C4

Pop and contemporary music often pushes these ranges, especially at the top, for expressive effect — but be mindful of the difference between an occasional peak note and making an entire chorus live at the top of the range.

### Instrumental Range Considerations

Each instrument has its own idiomatic range and characteristic tessitura. A melody written for guitar, trumpet, or violin needs to account for where each instrument speaks most naturally.

- Guitar melodic lines are often most comfortable between E2 and B4, with the most singing quality in the middle register.
- Trumpet speaks most brightly and easily from G3 to G5.
- Violin is rich from D4 upward, with an extensive range reaching to very high registers.
- Piano accommodates nearly any melodic range but has distinct character in different registers.

Beyond technical range, consider the character of the instrument in different registers. A cello melody in its lower register sounds dark and intimate; the same melody up an octave sounds warmer and more vocal. Use register as a compositional choice, not an afterthought.

## Rhythm in Melody

Rhythm is arguably as important as pitch in melody writing. Many of the most iconic melodic hooks are recognizable from rhythm alone before you even know what pitches are being played.

### Rhythmic Motifs

Just as a pitch motif is a short interval or pitch idea, a rhythmic motif is a distinctive rhythmic pattern that recurs through the melody. Define your rhythmic motif early and return to it consistently. This gives the melody coherence even when the pitch content is changing.

### Syncopation and Anticipation

Syncopation places rhythmic emphasis on off-beats or between the main beats. In pop, R&B, and funk especially, melody lines that anticipate the beat — landing on the "and" before the downbeat rather than on the downbeat itself — create a sense of forward momentum and groove.

Anticipated arrivals feel eagerly committed. Delayed arrivals feel held back, building tension. Knowing which effect you want tells you where to place your notes rhythmically.

### Rests and Space

Silence is part of the melody. Rests create breathing room, drama, and space for the listener to absorb what was just sung or played. They also create space for call-and-response (see above) and make returning melodic phrases feel like a re-entrance rather than a continuation.

A common mistake in melody writing is filling every moment with notes. Leave gaps. Let the phrase breathe. The note after the rest often carries more weight because of the silence before it.

### Delayed Resolution

Rhythmic delay in resolving a melodic line — arriving at the resolution later than expected — is a powerful tool for building tension. You know where the melody is going, the listener senses it, but you hold back. When the resolution finally arrives it feels earned.

This technique is closely related to the concept of the upward-resolving suspension in harmony: hold a note that wants to move, then let it move when the tension is at its peak.

## Melodic Phrasing and Breath Marks

A melody is made up of phrases — complete musical thoughts, analogous to sentences. Understanding phrase structure makes your melodies feel logical and coherent.

### Phrase Length

Most tonal melodies organize into two-bar or four-bar phrases. Four bars is the most common phrase unit in popular music. Eight bars is a common complete sentence (two four-bar phrases in antecedent-consequent relationship, roughly equivalent to a question and answer).

Varying phrase lengths creates interest. A series of four-bar phrases is stable but predictable. Insert a three-bar phrase or extend a phrase to five bars and the listener's ear is surprised — if it is done with intention, it feels organic rather than awkward.

### Antecedent and Consequent Phrases

The antecedent phrase (the "question") typically ends on a note of relative instability — the dominant (5), the supertonic (2), or some other non-tonic pitch. The consequent phrase (the "answer") completes the thought by ending on the tonic or another stable resolution.

This structure is deeply embedded in listeners' musical expectations. You can satisfy those expectations (resolution) or intentionally subvert them (unexpected continuation) for different emotional effects.

### Breath Marks for Vocalists

If you are writing for a voice, plan where the singer breathes. A phrase that runs too long without a natural breath point will either force the singer to interrupt an unexpected place or result in audible strain. Rests, long held notes (which allow a breath before the next phrase), and natural phrase endings are all places to build in breathing room.

Think about whether the lyric and the melody agree on where the breath should fall. A breath that interrupts an important lyrical word sounds awkward. A breath that follows a natural syntactic break in the text (end of a clause, a comma, a period) sounds natural.

## Pentatonic Melodies as a Starting Point

The pentatonic scale is one of the most universally used melodic resources across cultures and genres. It consists of five notes (in the major pentatonic: scale degrees 1, 2, 3, 5, 6) and has the remarkable property that every note sounds relatively consonant against common chord progressions.

### Why Pentatonic Works

The pentatonic scale omits the two notes most likely to clash with basic chord progressions (scale degrees 4 and 7). This makes it extremely forgiving — you can land on almost any note in the scale over almost any diatonic chord and it will sound reasonable. This is why beginners find it easier to improvise over a blues with a pentatonic scale than with a full scale.

For melody writing, this means the pentatonic gives you a safe palette to start generating melodic ideas without worrying too much about clashing with the harmony.

### Major Pentatonic

The major pentatonic (1, 2, 3, 5, 6) has a bright, open, optimistic character. Country, folk, gospel, and much of Western pop lean heavily on it. Think of the melody to "My Girl," "Let It Be," or countless folk songs.

### Minor Pentatonic

The minor pentatonic (1, b3, 4, 5, b7) has a darker, bluesier character. Rock, blues, and R&B melodies are often rooted in this scale. It also forms the basis of most blues improvisation.

### Starting a Melody Pentatonically

1. Pick a key and lay out the pentatonic scale.
2. Generate a melodic idea using only those five notes.
3. Once you have a shape you like, add chromatic notes selectively for color and tension.
4. The result is usually more refined and focused than starting from the full scale, where too many choices can be paralyzing.

### Moving Beyond the Pentatonic

The pentatonic is a starting point, not a ceiling. Once you have a pentatonic skeleton for your melody, add:

- The leading tone (7) for moments of strong resolution
- The fourth (4) as a tension note for color
- Chromatic passing tones (notes outside the key) for blues inflection, jazz color, or dramatic effect

Many classic melodies are essentially pentatonic with a few strategic additions. Identifying those additions in songs you love is a valuable ear training exercise.

## Putting It All Together

Strong melodies are not the result of following rules rigidly — they are the result of understanding principles deeply enough to apply them instinctively and break them purposefully.

A practical process for writing a melody:

1. **Define the emotional intention.** What should this melody make the listener feel? That answer guides contour, register, interval choices, and rhythm.

2. **Sketch the contour.** Before writing notes, draw the shape. Where does it peak? Does it arch, ascend, or do something else?

3. **Start with a motif.** Two to four notes with a distinctive rhythm. Everything else can grow from there.

4. **Use the pentatonic to generate ideas freely.** Then refine and add color.

5. **Check the tension and release map.** Where are the tension points? Where do they resolve? Is the arc satisfying?

6. **Sing it.** Everything. All the time. If you cannot sing it, your intended performer may struggle too. Singing also reveals whether a melody is genuinely memorable or just comfortable in the moment.

7. **Develop the motif.** Apply sequence, inversion, augmentation. See what variations reveal.

8. **Check phrasing and breath.** Does it breathe naturally? Does the phrase structure make musical sense?

9. **Map it against the harmony.** Are your tension notes intentional? Do they resolve well?

10. **Leave it alone and come back.** A melody that is still in your head tomorrow is a melody worth using.

The goal is a melody so well-crafted that it sounds inevitable — as if it could not have been any other way. That sense of inevitability is not accidental. It is the result of a lot of deliberate, patient, playful work.
