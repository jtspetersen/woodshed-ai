# Guitar Chord Voicings: A Comprehensive Reference

A chord voicing is the specific arrangement of notes you choose to play — which strings, which frets, which octaves. Two guitarists can play a "C major chord" and sound completely different from each other based entirely on voicing choices. Understanding voicings transforms you from someone who plays chords to someone who shapes sound, serves a song, and communicates musically with other players.

This reference covers the major voicing families available on guitar, from first-position open chords all the way to rootless jazz structures, with practical tips on when to use each and how to move between them smoothly.

---

## The CAGED System: Your Fretboard Map

Before diving into specific voicing types, the CAGED system gives you a framework for understanding where every chord lives on the neck. The name comes from five open-chord shapes — C, A, G, E, and D — that, when connected, tile the entire fretboard in repeating patterns.

### How CAGED Works

Each shape is named after the open chord it resembles when played without a capo or barre. As you move up the neck, you shift from one shape to the next, always in the order C → A → G → E → D → C (repeating). Every key has all five shapes available, and they overlap so that the top strings of one shape become the bottom strings of the next.

For C major, the five shapes fall roughly here:

| Shape | Root Position (C major) | Lowest Root Fret |
|-------|------------------------|-----------------|
| C     | Open position          | 5th string, 3rd fret |
| A     | 3rd fret barre          | 5th string, 3rd fret |
| G     | 5th fret area           | 6th string, 8th fret |
| E     | 8th fret barre          | 6th string, 8th fret |
| D     | 10th fret area          | 4th string, 10th fret |

### Why CAGED Matters for Voicings

CAGED is not just about chord shapes — it maps out where your chord tones (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th) live relative to any given shape. Once you know which notes are which within a shape, you can alter, extend, or omit specific tones to create any voicing you need. It also makes transposing effortless: learn a voicing in one shape and you can move it anywhere on the neck.

## Finding Chord Tones on the Fretboard

To build and modify voicings intelligently, you need to know where your chord tones live. There are two practical approaches.

### The Low E and A String Root System

Most guitarists start by memorizing notes on the 6th (low E) and 5th (A) strings, since these strings anchor the majority of barre chords and jazz shapes. From a root on the 6th string, the major triad tones are:

- **Root** — on the 6th string (your reference)
- **Major 3rd** — two strings up, two frets up (on the 4th string)
- **Perfect 5th** — two strings up, two frets down from the 3rd (on the 4th string, one fret down)

A more visual approach: in an E-shape barre chord at fret X, the root is on string 6 at fret X, and also on string 1 at fret X, string 4 at fret X+2.

### Interval Shapes (String-to-String)

Intervals have consistent shapes across adjacent strings (with a slight exception when crossing from string 3 to string 2, where the tuning shifts from a perfect 4th to a major 3rd):

- **Octave**: same string, 12 frets up — or two strings up, two frets back (on non-B-string pairs)
- **Major 3rd**: one string up, one fret back (with B-string correction: two frets back)
- **Perfect 5th**: one string up, two frets forward

Memorizing these shapes lets you quickly find any chord tone relative to any note you already know.

## Open Position Chords (Cowboy Chords)

Open position chords use at least one open string and are played in the first few frets. They are the foundation of folk, country, rock, blues, and pop guitar.

### When to Use Them

Open chords are ideal when you want resonance and sustain (open strings ring freely), when you want a full, piano-like sound in acoustic settings, and when the song is in a guitar-friendly key (E, A, D, G, C, or their relative minors). They also work beautifully when the arrangement has space — a single acoustic guitar accompanying a singer benefits enormously from the natural reverb of open strings.

### Essential Open Chord Shapes

Fret numbers are given string by string from the low E (6th string) to high E (1st string). "x" means muted/not played, "0" means open.

**E major**: `0-2-2-1-0-0`
**E minor**: `0-2-2-0-0-0`
**A major**: `x-0-2-2-2-0`
**A minor**: `x-0-2-2-1-0`
**D major**: `x-x-0-2-3-2`
**D minor**: `x-x-0-2-3-1`
**G major**: `3-2-0-0-0-3` (or `3-2-0-0-3-3`)
**C major**: `x-3-2-0-1-0`
**F major (partial)**: `x-x-3-2-1-1` (economical version)

### Voice Leading Tips for Open Chords

The classic I-IV-V movement in G (G-C-D) can feel clunky if you just block-shift between shapes. Look for common tones to anchor your ear:

- G to C: the G note (open 3rd string) remains a common tone. Keep it ringing as you move to C shape.
- C to Am: three of four fretted notes stay the same — only the bass note changes.
- In the key of D, moving D-G-A works smoothly because the high B string (string 2) stays open across all three chords.

Whenever two adjacent chords share a note, let it sustain through the change. The listener's ear hears continuity, and the transition sounds musical rather than mechanical.

## Barre Chords: E-Shape and A-Shape

Barre chords use the index finger to fret all six (or five) strings at a single fret, effectively acting as a movable capo. Combined with the CAGED system, two shapes — E-form and A-form — cover the entire fretboard for both major and minor chords in any key.

### When to Use Them

Barre chords are the workhorses of rock, pop, punk, reggae, and funk. They allow you to play any chord at any position with a consistent tone and attack, without relying on open strings (which can sound out of place in some arrangements). They also make transposing trivial: if you know the F barre chord (E-shape at fret 1), you already know every major chord up the neck.

### E-Shape Barre Chords

The E-shape is derived from the open E major/minor chord. Barre the index finger across all six strings at the target fret, then form the remaining fingers as you would for open E.

**Root on the 6th string. To find the chord: the root note is whatever pitch is on the 6th string at the barre fret.**

**E-shape major barre at fret 5 (A major)**:
`5-7-7-6-5-5`
Index finger: fret 5 across all strings
Middle, ring, pinky: frets 7-7-6 on strings 4-3-2

**E-shape minor barre at fret 5 (A minor)**:
`5-7-7-5-5-5`
Index finger: fret 5, remaining fingers cover frets 7-7 on strings 4-3 only

**Barre chord roots on the 6th string** (common positions):
- Fret 1: F
- Fret 3: G
- Fret 5: A
- Fret 7: B
- Fret 8: C
- Fret 10: D

### A-Shape Barre Chords

The A-shape uses the open A chord as its template, with the root on the 5th string.

**A-shape major barre at fret 3 (C major)**:
`x-3-5-5-5-3`
Index finger: fret 3 across strings 5-1
Ring or pinky: frets 5-5-5 on strings 4-3-2 (or barre with ring finger)

**A-shape minor barre at fret 3 (C minor)**:
`x-3-5-5-4-3`

**Barre chord roots on the 5th string** (common positions):
- Fret 0 (open): A
- Fret 2: B
- Fret 3: C
- Fret 5: D
- Fret 7: E
- Fret 8: F
- Fret 10: G

### Voice Leading Tips for Barre Chords

The challenge with barre chords is that every shape shift feels like a full chord move — there are no open strings providing continuity. A few strategies help:

- **Stay in position when possible.** For a I-IV progression in A (A to D), the A-shape A chord is at fret 5, 5th string. The D chord as an A-shape barre is at fret 5, 6th string — you barely move.
- **Connect shapes that share notes on adjacent strings.** The top two strings of an E-shape often match the top two strings of the A-shape one position up, creating a pivot point.
- **Use the same shape type.** Moving between two E-shape chords along the neck is smoother than alternating E-shape and A-shape, because your finger geometry doesn't change.

## Power Chords

Power chords contain only the root and the perfect 5th (sometimes doubled at the octave), omitting the 3rd entirely. Without a 3rd, they are technically neither major nor minor — they are ambiguous, raw, and punchy.

### When to Use Them

Power chords are the defining sound of rock and metal. They cut through distortion better than full chords because the absence of the 3rd means less harmonic complexity to muddy under high gain. They are also used in punk, alternative, and even some funk and pop contexts for emphasis.

### Common Power Chord Shapes

**Root on 6th string (5th-string 5th)**:
`X-5-7-7-x-x` (D5, root at fret 5, 6th string)

The standard two-finger power chord: `x-5-7-x-x-x`
Root + 5th, no doubling.

Three-note power chord with octave: `x-5-7-7-x-x`
Root + 5th + root an octave up.

**Root on 5th string**:
`x-x-5-7-7-x` (G5, root at fret 5, 5th string)

### Voice Leading Tips for Power Chords

Power chord progressions live and die by their bass movement. The most effective technique is treating the root as a bass line and making that movement melodic:

- **Chromatic approach**: slide into the target chord from a half step below. Playing E5 to F5, briefly stop on E5 then slide to F5 on the downbeat — the approach note creates tension that releases satisfyingly.
- **Contrary motion with a lead line**: pair a descending power chord progression with an ascending guitar lick or lead melody. The tension between the two directions creates energy.

## Jazz Voicings: Drop 2, Drop 3, and Rootless

Jazz chords on guitar typically use four or five strings and involve 7th chords (major 7, dominant 7, minor 7) as basic vocabulary, with extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) added for color. The concepts of "drop 2" and "drop 3" describe specific ways of spreading these chord tones across the strings.

### Understanding Drop Voicings

A "closed" voicing stacks chord tones as close together as possible (like a piano chord in the left hand). A "drop 2" voicing takes a closed 4-note chord and drops the second-highest note down an octave. A "drop 3" drops the third-highest note down an octave.

On guitar, these transformations produce the most playable and characteristic jazz chord shapes.

### Drop 2 Voicings

Drop 2 voicings are the bread and butter of jazz guitar. They sit on adjacent string sets (strings 6-5-4-3, 5-4-3-2, or 4-3-2-1) and produce open, clear-sounding chords.

**Cmaj7 drop 2 on strings 4-3-2-1 (root position)**:
`x-x-3-4-5-3` (frets: x-x-3-4-5-3)
Chord tones from string 4 to 1: C (root), E (3rd), B (major 7th), G (5th)

**G7 drop 2 on strings 4-3-2-1**:
`x-x-5-4-3-3`
Chord tones: G, B, F, D (root, 3rd, b7, 5th)

**Dm7 drop 2 on strings 4-3-2-1**:
`x-x-0-2-1-1` or at position: `x-x-5-7-6-5`

Drop 2 voicings are most effective when comping behind a soloist — they sit in the midrange without competing with the bass and leave sonic space above for the melody.

### Drop 3 Voicings

Drop 3 voicings use a wider string spread (typically skipping a string, e.g., strings 6-4-3-2) and produce a deeper, more spacious sound.

**Cmaj7 drop 3 on strings 5-4-3-2**:
`x-3-2-4-5-x`

**G7 drop 3 on strings 6-4-3-2**:
`3-x-4-3-3-x`

Drop 3 shapes are great for chord-melody playing, where the guitar must cover bass, harmony, and melody simultaneously. The wider voicing gives each register room to breathe.

### Rootless Voicings

In a band or ensemble context, the bassist plays the root. Guitarists and pianists often drop the root from their voicings to create lighter, more harmonically sophisticated sounds that blend rather than clutter.

A rootless Cmaj7 might include only E-B-G (3rd, major 7th, 5th), or the guide tones E and B with an added 9th (D) or 13th (A).

**Rootless Cmaj9 on strings 4-3-2-1**:
`x-x-2-4-5-3` (E-B-D-G = 3rd, maj7, 9th, 5th)

**Rootless G13 on strings 4-3-2-1**:
`x-x-5-4-5-3` (B-F-E-D = 3rd, b7, 13th, 9th)

### Voice Leading in Jazz Voicings

Jazz voice leading is about keeping each voice (each individual note in the chord) moving by the smallest possible interval. This minimizes jumpy or angular harmonic movement.

**The ii-V-I in C (Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7)** is the most important jazz progression. Notice how the guide tones (3rds and 7ths) resolve:

- Dm7: F (3rd) and C (b7)
- G7: F (7th) and B (3rd) — F stays, C moves down a half step to B
- Cmaj7: E (3rd) and B (maj7) — F moves down a half step to E, B stays

This half-step resolution of the tritone (F and B in G7) into a stable major 7th chord (E and B in Cmaj7) is the engine of jazz harmony. Keep it in ear and in hand.

## Slash Chords and Inversions

A slash chord is written as "chord/bass note" — for example, C/E means a C major chord with E in the bass. On guitar, this means finding a voicing where the lowest sounding string plays the specified bass note.

### When to Use Them

Slash chords are used to create smooth, stepwise bass lines under otherwise static harmony, to create tension and color, and to specify inversions for precise harmonic effect.

**First inversion (3rd in bass)**: C/E
`0-3-2-0-1-0` — standard C shape with the open low E string ringing as the bass

**Second inversion (5th in bass)**: C/G
`3-3-2-0-1-0` — C shape with G on the 6th string at fret 3

**Common slash chord applications**:

- **G/B**: `x-2-0-0-0-3` — moves the bass from G down to B, creating a descending line
- **D/F#**: `2-0-0-2-3-2` — places the major 3rd in the bass; common in the progression G-D/F#-Em
- **Am/G**: `3-0-2-2-1-0` — adds the subtonic in the bass, creates a G-pedal effect under Am

### Building Descending Bass Lines with Slash Chords

One of the most powerful uses of slash chords is creating a chromatic or stepwise bass descent while the upper harmony stays relatively stable. The classic example in rock and pop:

**C — C/B — Am — Am/G** (bass descends: C-B-A-G)
`x-3-2-0-1-0` → `x-2-2-0-1-0` → `x-0-2-2-1-0` → `3-0-2-2-1-0`

This kind of bass movement mimics a cello or bass counterline and adds tremendous gravity and momentum to an otherwise simple progression.

## Partial Chord Shapes: Triads on String Sets

Full six-string chords can overwhelm an ensemble or clutter a mix. Partial chord shapes — three-string triads — cut through cleanly, leave space for other instruments, and unlock highly mobile, precise voicing choices.

### When to Use Them

Triads on string sets are essential in:

- **Band contexts** where a bassist covers the low end and you need to sit in the middle register
- **Funk and R&B** where rhythmic chops on high strings complement a thick bass
- **Country** where the bright, trebly twang of high-string triads defines the genre's guitar sound
- **Chord melody arrangements** where clarity matters over fullness

### Triad Shapes on Strings 1-2-3 (High String Set)

**Major triads on strings 1-2-3** (root position, first inversion, second inversion):

Root position (root on top):
- C major (root C on string 1): `x-x-x-5-5-5` (frets G-C-E on strings 3-2-1, first inversion shape) — *correction:* root position has root as the lowest note.

Let's use practical, movable shapes. Here the number is the fret for strings 3-2-1:

**Major triad, root position** (root on string 3): `0-1-0` shape (fret offset from root)
Example: G major at fret 12: `12-12-12` is not quite right — let's use fixed examples.

**C major triads on strings 3-2-1**:
- Root position (C on string 3, fret 5): `5-5-5`
- First inversion (E on string 3, fret 9): `9-8-8`
- Second inversion (G on string 3, fret 12): `12-13-12`

**C minor triads on strings 3-2-1**:
- Root position: `5-4-5`
- First inversion: `8-8-8`
- Second inversion: `12-13-11`

### Triad Shapes on Strings 2-3-4

These sit in the midrange and blend well when comping. They are especially effective in jazz and soul contexts.

**D major triads on strings 4-3-2**:
- Root position (D on string 4, fret 0): `0-2-3`
- First inversion (F# on string 4, fret 4): `4-3-3`
- Second inversion (A on string 4, fret 7): `7-7-7`

### Voice Leading with Triads

The real power of learning all three inversions is smooth, horizontal movement up the neck. Instead of jumping from one position to another, you shift to the nearest inversion:

**C major → F major on strings 1-2-3** (close voice leading):
- C (root position, fret 5-5-5 strings 3-2-1): `5-5-5`
- F (second inversion, fret 5-6-5 strings 3-2-1): `5-6-5`

Only one note moves — the B moves up one fret to C. This is voice leading at its most efficient.

**Diatonic triads ascending the G major scale on strings 1-2-3**:
G: `4-3-3` (strings 3-2-1, first inversion) → A min: `5-3-3` → B min: `7-4-3` → C: `5-5-5` (root position) → D: `7-7-7` (second inversion) → E min: `9-8-8` → F# dim: `10-10-9`

This sequence, once under your fingers, opens up an entire vocabulary of moving lines and harmonic color.

## Common Chord Movement Patterns

### Chromatic Bass Lines

A chromatic bass line moves the lowest note in half steps while the upper voices remain static or change minimally. The effect is forward momentum and a sense of searching or descending gravity.

**Classic example: descending chromatic from I to IV**
In the key of E: `E — E/D# — E/D — A/C#`
`0-2-2-1-0-0` → `x-4-2-2-0-0` → `x-3-2-2-0-0` → `x-4-2-2-2-0`

The bass moves E-D#-D-C# while the upper chord largely stays as E major flavor, then lands on A/C# for arrival.

### Pedal Tones

A pedal tone holds a single bass note constant while the harmony above it changes. On guitar, open strings make natural pedal tones because they ring freely regardless of what your fretting hand is doing.

**D pedal (open 4th string) under diatonic chords**:
D (open): `x-x-0-2-3-2`
G/D: `x-x-0-0-0-3`
A/D: `x-0-0-2-2-0`
Bm/D: `x-x-0-4-3-2`

The constant D in the bass grounds the progression even as the upper harmony floats through the diatonic scale.

**High string pedal tone (string 1 open E)**:
Many fingerpickers use the open first string as a persistent high E drone. Chords are voiced on strings 2-6 while the high E rings throughout, creating a shimmering, modal quality common in Celtic, Appalachian, and contemporary fingerstyle.

### Chromatically Approaching Target Chords

Rather than landing directly on a chord, approach it from a half step below or above. This device is borrowed from jazz but works in blues, rock, and R&B:

**Approaching D major from below (C# major → D major)**:
`x-4-6-6-6-4` → `x-5-7-7-7-5`

The single-fret slide up creates anticipation and release. Approach chords are typically played quickly — a sixteenth note before the downbeat landing.

## Bringing It All Together: Choosing the Right Voicing

Every voicing decision is a musical decision. Ask yourself:

**Where does this chord need to sit in the mix?** Full six-string open chords fill the entire frequency range. Jazz triads on strings 1-2-3 live in the treble. Drop 2 voicings occupy the middle. Match your voicing register to the sonic space available.

**Is the bassist playing the root?** If yes, consider rootless or high-register voicings. If you are playing solo or your bass player is taking a break, make sure your voicing implies the bass note clearly.

**How much movement is needed between chords?** For smooth, flowing progressions, use voice leading — keep notes in common, resolve half steps where they exist. For dramatic, powerful shifts, big chord moves (jumping shapes or positions) create impact.

**What genre and feel does the song need?** Open cowboy chords evoke campfire acoustic warmth. Barre chords with distortion evoke power and aggression. Drop 2 jazz voicings evoke sophistication and restraint. Triads on the high strings evoke brightness and precision.

The fretboard is not a collection of separate boxes — the CAGED system, the chord tone shapes, and the voicing types all overlap and connect into a continuous, mappable surface. Every chord you know in one position exists in four others. Every voicing type can be found on multiple string sets. The more you internalize this connectivity, the more freely you move, and the more musically you play.

Practice one voicing type at a time, through all keys, until it feels as natural as breathing. Then connect it to the adjacent types — barre to partial triads, open chords to slash inversions, drop 2 to rootless. Over time, chord voicings stop being things you look up and become a fluid vocabulary you speak in real time.
